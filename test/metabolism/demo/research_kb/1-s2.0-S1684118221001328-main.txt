【摘要 / Abstract】
----------------------------------------------------------------------
Clostridioides difﬁcile is one of the most common nosocomial gastrointestinal
pathogens, and recurrence is a problematic issue because approximately 20e30% of patients
experience at least one episode of recurrence, even after treatment with a therapeutic drug
of choice for C. difﬁcile infection (CDI), such as vancomycin. CDI recurrence has a multifactorial complex mechanism, in which gut microbiota disruption coincident with viable C. difﬁcile
spores, is considered the most important factor. The effectiveness of an anti-C. difﬁcile antimicrobial agent against CDI cannot guarantee its inhibitory effect on C. difﬁcile spores and
vice versa. However, an antimicrobial agent, such as ﬁdaxomicin, which has a good inhibitory
effect on both C. difﬁcile vegetative cells and spores is assumed to not only treat CDI but also
prevent its recurrence. Prolonged adherence to the exosporium has been proposed as a
possible mechanism of inhibiting spores, and as a result, redesigning anti-C. difﬁcile antimicrobial agents with the ability to adhere to the exosporium may provide another pathway for the
development of anti-C. difﬁcile spore agents. For example, vancomycin lacks an inhibitory effect against C. difﬁcile spores, but a vancomycin-loaded spore-targeting iron oxide nanoparticle that selectively binds to C. difﬁcile spores has been developed to successfully delay
spore germination. Some new antimicrobial agents in phase II clinical trials, including cadazolid and ridinilazole, have shown exceptional anti-C. difﬁcile and spore-inhibiting effects that
can be expected to not only treat CDI but also prevent its recurrence in the future.

Introduction
Clostridioides difﬁcile is one of the most common nosocomial gastrointestinal pathogens, with clinical presentations
ranging from mild diarrhea and pseudomembranous colitis
to toxic megacolon, with a mortality of up to 25e40%.1e9
Severe colitis develops in approximately 3% of patients
with C. difﬁcile infection (CDI) who may have paradoxical
decrease in the amount of diarrhea after ileus or toxic
megacolon developed.3,10 Diagnosis of CDI relies on the
detection of toxigenic C. difﬁcile, its toxins or toxin genes
in stool.9,11
Metronidazole was suggested for mild CDI and vancomycin was recommended for severe CDI.8 Nevertheless,
oral vancomycin and ﬁdaxomicin have been suggested to
replace metronidazole as the therapeutic choice for either
mild-moderate or severe CDI in the Clinical Practice
Guidelines for C. difﬁcile Infection in Adults and Children:
2017 Update by the Infectious Diseases Society of America
(IDSA) and Society for Healthcare Epidemiology of America
(SHEA).12 For patients with severe and fulminant forms of
CDI presenting as ileus, oral intake of vancomycin or
ﬁdaxomicin is disrupted because of the obstruction of the
gut because ﬁdaxomicin can only be administered orally;
and
vancomycin, either administered orally or
intrarectally. This leads to marked disruption of the microbiota,
leading to prolonged loss of colonization resistance to C.
difﬁcile and dense colonization by vancomycin-resistant
Enterococcus, K. pneumoniae, and E. coli.13
Although antimicrobial resistance is not clinically problematic, treating CDI with metronidazole and vancomycin is
associated with a high treatment failure rate and high
relapse rate.14 According to the Clinical Practice Guidelines
by IDSA/SHEA, oral vancomycin was still the drug of choice
for recurrent C. difﬁcile associated diarrhea, but the rate
of second recurrence rate is as high as 22.6e41.8%, despite
successful treatment with vancomycin.15 The high recurrence rate of CDI after treatment might be due to the
generation of spores that lead to persistence of CDI.16,17
The biology of spores in the pathogenesis of C.
difﬁcile
Owing to its strict anaerobic requirements, the infectious
and transmissible morphotype of C. difﬁcile is a dormant
spore, which is characterized by resistance to heat, oxygen, and common disinfectants, such as ethanol-based
hand sanitizers.16,18,19 The sporeeresistance properties
are attributed to a low water content (25e60% of wet
weight), elevated levels of dipicolonic acid (25% of core dry
weight) chelated with calcium, and the saturation of DNA
with a/b-type small acid soluble proteins.16,20
The spores of C. difﬁcile has the innermost spore
compartment known as the spore core, which contains the
spore DNA, RNA and most enzymes.16 The spore core is
encased in three protective layers: the ﬁrst is the compressed inner membrane protein surrounded by the germ
cell wall, which has a very low permeability to small molecules, including water, and protects the core from DNAdamaging molecules, the second is the peptidoglycan cortex, a coat that predominantly consists of proteins, and the
third is the exosporium (the outermost layer), which predominantly consists of glycoproteins.16
During CDI, C. difﬁcile initiates a sporulation pathway
that produces dormant spores that lead to persistence and
dissemination of CDI within hospitalized patients, and the
pathway is triggered by environmental stimuli such as
nutrient starvation, quorum sensing and other unidentiﬁed
stress factors.16,17 When C. difﬁcile spores are ingested,
the spores are capable of enduring the acidic conditions of
the stomach and then transition to a metabolically active
state (germinate) in the small intestine.21 Germination of
C. difﬁcile spores and the growth of vegetative forms take
place only in the lower gastrointestinal tract where the
oxygen concentration is very low.22,23 Some contents of the
intestine, such as bile acids, induce germination of the
spores into an actively replicating vegetative cell.23,24
Taurocholate, a conjugated primary bile acid in the small
intestine, has the ability to promote germination and to
inﬂuence vegetative growth of C. difﬁcile; in contrast,
chenodeoxycholate, another primary bile acid, inhibits
spore germination in C. difﬁcile and acts both as a
competitive inhibitor of taurocholate and a suppressor of
vegetative growth.24,25 The efﬁciency of germination varies
between clinical strains of C. difﬁcile and whether it is
associated with ribotype, disease severity or therapeutic
failure needs further investigation.23,26
Rationale of the implication of spore inhibition
for the prevention of recurrence of C. difﬁcile
infection: is it the absolute and only solution?
Recurrence is a problematic issue in CDI, with 20e30% of
patients facing at least one episode of recurrence even
when treated with a therapeutic drug of choice for CDI.21,27
In a simulated CDI gut model, although vancomycin successfully treated the initial episode of CDI, recurrence
occurred approximately 18 days after therapy cessation, in
which the germination of spores of C. difﬁcile in the environment of a bioﬁlm was noted.28 The recurrence rates of
clinical patients with CDI receiving metronidazole and
vancomycin therapy, two of the most commonly used antiC. difﬁcile antibiotics, were 27.1% and 24.0%, respectively.27 The recurrence could be due to either reinfection

of a susceptible individual or reactivation of latent spores
within the individual’s gastrointestinal tract.27
CDI recurrence has a multi-factorial complex mechanism, but gut microbiota disruption coincident with viable
C. difﬁcile spores is considered as the most important
factor.28,29 Of course, targeting spores of C. difﬁcile is not
the only way to resolve the troublesome CDI recurrence
problem; multiple factors, including gut microbiota status
and the ribotype of the C. difﬁcile (with different spore
germination efﬁciency) also inﬂuences the recurrence rate
of CDI.26 For example, rifaximin, though lacking sporeinhibiting effects, can prevent the recurrence of CDI
when given immediately after completing standard anti-CDI
antibiotics through its relative sparing of enteric microbiota.30e32 The ribotype of C. difﬁcile with different spore
germination efﬁciencies will inﬂuence the recurrent rate of
CDI; for example, ribotype 027 isolates exhibit signiﬁcantly
higher germination efﬁciencies in the presence of sodium
taurocholate than ribotype 106 and ribotype 078.26
Even with the inﬂuence of the gut microbiota condition
and the ribotype of CDI, developing antimicrobial agents
with both anti-C. difﬁcile and spore-inhibiting effects
have been proven to provide a way to not only treat CDI
but also prevent its recurrence, including ﬁdaxomicin, one
of the drugs of choice for CDI, and cadazolid and ridinilazole, two antimicrobial agents still in phase II clinical
trials (Table 1).33e37
The application of the spore-inhibiting effect
of current anti-C. difﬁcile antimicrobial agents
in the therapy of C. difﬁcile infection and its
recurrence
The effectiveness of an anti-C. difﬁcile antimicrobial agent
toward CDI cannot guarantee its inhibitory effect on spores
of C. difﬁcile and vice versa.38e40 Though many antimicrobial agents are regarded as drugs of choice for CDI, such
as ﬁdaxomicin and vancomycin, their inhibitory effects on
C. difﬁcile spores are diverse, which might be due to their
different adherent and inhibitory effects toward spores of
C. difﬁcile38,39; in contrast, tigecycline and piperacillin/
tazobactam, two commonly used antimicrobial agents in
the clinic, both have a dose-dependent inhibitory effect on
spore production at subminimum inhibitory concentrations
(sub-MICs) in-vitro, but their clinical application is limited
because
of
their
inadequate
therapeutic
effect
on
CDI.16,40,41 If an antimicrobial agent, such as ﬁdaxomicin,
has both anti-vegetative cell and anti-spore effects toward
C. difﬁcile, it is assumed to be able to not only achieve a
therapeutic effect but also lower the recurrence rate.33 In
contrast, if an antimicrobial agent, such as vancomycin, has
only anti-vegetative cell effects but lacks anti-spore effects
toward C. difﬁcile, it will be hypothetically only achieving a
therapeutic effect but with a possible high recurrence rate
after therapy unless there is some modiﬁcation of the
antimicrobial agents to strengthen its effect on spores.38,39
Fidaxomicin, a drug used for CDI therapy, can inhibit both
the sporulation and the outgrowth of a vegetative cell from
the germinating spore, but its effect on the germination of
C. difﬁcile spores is limited (Table 1).30,42e45 Fidaxomicin
has a persistent inhibitory effect on sporulation when added
to
early-stationary-phase
cells
in
C.
difﬁcile
strains,
including the epidemic NAP1/BI/027 strain at sub-MICs, and
thus prevents subsequent growth and toxin production invitro; in contrast, vancomycin and metronidazole do not
inhibit sporulation at similar sub-MICs.30,43,44 In contrast to
its inhibitory effect on sporulation, ﬁdaxomicin was found to
have no inhibitory effect on the initiation of C. difﬁcile
spore germination by analyzing the kinetics of the initiation
of spore germination.42
The spore-inhibiting effect of ﬁdaxomicin has been
illustrated in clinical trials, although the clinical cure rates
with ﬁdaxomicin were similar to those with vancomycin in
both the modiﬁed intention-to-treat analysis (88.2% with
ﬁdaxomicin and 85.8% with vancomycin) and per-protocol
analysis (92.1% and 89.8%, respectively), signiﬁcantly fewer
patients in the ﬁdaxomicin group than in the vancomycin
group had a recurrence of the infection, in both the modiﬁed intention-to-treat analysis (15.4% vs. 25.3%, P Z 0.005)
and per-protocol analysis (13.3% vs. 24.0%, P Z 0.004).46
The lower recurrence rate following ﬁdaxomicin therapy
compared to vancomycin was also due to its lower effect on
the microbiota.15,47e50 In the mice model, ﬁdaxomicin
reduced the proportion of Clostridial growth to a lesser
extent, but increased that of Bacteroidia, resulting in less
disturbance in microbiota diversity.47 The lesser inﬂuence
on the microbiota composition of ﬁdaxomicin, compared to
vancomycin, promoted faster microbial recovery in the gut,
and had more colonization resistance to C. difﬁcile.48e50
Prolonged adherence to the exosporium has been proposed as a possible way of inhibiting spores; as a result,
redesigning anti-C. difﬁcile antimicrobial agents with the
ability to adhere to the exosporium may provide another
pathway for the development of anti-C. difﬁcile spore
agents.38,39 Vancomycin, although considered as one of the
drugs of choice for CDI, was found to have no inhibitory
effect on the initiation of C. difﬁcile spore germination by
analyzing the kinetics of the initiation of spore germination.42 Thus, the recurrence rate after vancomycin therapy
was as high as 24.0%.27 although vancomycin itself has no
effect on C. difﬁcile spores, a vancomycin-loaded sporetargeting iron oxide nanoparticle (van-IONP) that selectively binds to C. difﬁcile spores has been developed to
successfully delay spore germination.38
Potential future armament for inhibiting C.
difﬁcile spores and preventing recurrence of
C. difﬁcile infection
Some antimicrobial agents undergoing clinical trials have
shown excellent anti-C. difﬁcile and spore-inhibiting effects,
which can not only treat CDI but also prevent its recurrence

Cadazolid,
a
novel
ﬂuoroquinoloneoxazolidinone antibiotic in phase II and III trials currently,
exhibits potent in-vitro activity against C. difﬁcile and its
spores,33,34 and has limited effect on the gut microﬂora,
including the B. fragilis group and Lactobacillus spp.51,52 The
cure rates of patients with CDI that received 250, 500, or
1000 mg cadazolid twice daily were 76.5%, 80.0%, 68.4%,
respectively, compared to 68.2% in the group treated with
125 mg vancomycin four times daily; in addition, each

including several Bacteroidaceae and Clostridiaceae families, and maintained the intestinal bile acid proﬁle.35,55 In
phase II trials, the sustained clinical response in patients in
the ridinilazole group was 66.7%, which was superior to that
of 42.4% in the vancomycin group.35 Of note, the recurrence
rate of CDI among patients receiving ridinilazole was 14.3%,
which was lower than that of 34.8% for vancomycin.35

CRS3123, a fully synthetic diaryldiamine antimicrobial
drug, prevents both C. difﬁcile growth and spore production by inhibiting bacterial protein synthesis, with a >10fold reduction in the sporulation rate in-vitro.56 To date,
only data from a phase I study assessing the safety and
tolerability of CRS3123 are available, and evidence of its
clinical effect on CDI and its recurrence is deﬁcient.32
Nisin, a bacteriocin produced by several strains of Lactococcus lactis, was noted to have inhibitory activity
against clinical isolates of C. difﬁcile, with spore viability
reduced by 40e50% in-vitro.57 However, in-vivo evidence of
nisin in treating CDI or its recurrence is absent.
Development of a vaccine targeting the spores of C.
difﬁcile provides a feasible pathway for preventing CDI
recurrence.58 Anti-spore IgY from chickens delayed the
occurrence of diarrhea by 1.5 d and the spore adherence to
the colonic mucosa by 90% in a mouse model, and coadministration of anti-spore IgY coupled with vancomycin
delayed the appearance of recurrent diarrhea by a median
of 2 days.58
Some small molecules and compounds were noted to
have spore-inhibiting effects in-vitro, but a determination
of their “real” clinical efﬁcacy needs further clinical trials.59,60 Carvacrol, a food-grade, plant-derived compound,
signiﬁcantly reduces sporulation, germination and spore
outgrowth of C. difﬁcile and downregulates critical genes
involved in spore production in-vitro.59 Rhodomyrtone, a
bioactive compound derived from the leaves of the rose
myrtle (Rhodomyrtus tomentosa), is four-fold more potent
than vancomycin in inhibiting C. difﬁcile spore outgrowth
in-vitro.60
Conclusion
Developing an antimicrobial agent with a good inhibitory
effect on both C. difﬁcile vegetative cells and spores can
be expected to not only treat CDI but also prevent its
recurrence, such as ﬁdaxomicin. Cadazolid and ridinilazole
possess a spore-inhibiting effect in-vitro, and thus decrease
the recurrence of CDI after cessation of the drug in phase II
and III clinical trials. Some small molecules and compounds
were noted to have spore-inhibiting effects in-vitro, but the
determination of their “real” clinical efﬁcacy requires
further clinical trials.
